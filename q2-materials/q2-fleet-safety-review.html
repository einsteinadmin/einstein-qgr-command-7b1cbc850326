The one number that went up

Yes, raw safety events rose to 902 (from 672). Here's why that's not the story it looks like.

We ran 23.5% more miles and 21.8% more hours than Q1 with a fleet at full summer capacity and a wave of new drivers. More trucks moving more often naturally surfaces more events — and it's exactly what we planned for.

More importantly, we tightened our own standards twice — on Rolling Stops and Inattentive Driving. Those two categories alone account for 180 of the 230-event increase (78%). We didn't get less safe; we started counting things we used to let slide.

Hold those two categories at Q1's stricter-but-not-strictest level and Q2 lands at 722 events — a 13% lower event rate per mile than Q1. The underlying driving got safer while the fleet got busier.

Dismissal integrity review

We audited what we're dismissing — and closed the one real gap

A lower dismissal rate only counts for something if the alerts we do wave off actually deserve it. So we went alert-by-alert across every branch to pressure-test the calls sitting behind the number.

Bottom line: the large majority of dismissals are legitimate.

Most land in three defensible buckets — early or completed rolling stops, following distance that self-corrected, and system or hardware faults. The one genuinely soft category was iPad-related Inattentive Driving, and that gap is already closed with a new standard set this week.

The company-wide pattern: Rolling Stop

Rolling Stop was the near-universal dismissal — it showed up at 6 of 7 branches, with the same defensible reason everywhere: the driver stopped a touch early or just past the sign but did come to a full stop, or the camera false-flagged off a different sign.

The bottom line for ownership

  • We are demonstrably safer. Score +8.8 points, high-risk drivers cut nearly in half, dismissal rate down to 20.7% from 48.2% two quarters ago.
  • The event increase is a volume-and-standards story, not a safety regression. Normalized for the standards we tightened, our per-mile event rate actually fell 13%.
  • Our own accountability is tightening. We're dismissing far fewer alerts and holding stricter thresholds on rolling stops and inattentive driving — the numbers reflect a higher bar, not worse driving.
  • We didn't just trust the dismissal number — we audited it. An alert-by-alert review across every branch found the large majority legitimate (early/complete rolling stops, self-corrected following distance, hardware faults). The one soft spot — iPad-looking Inattentive alerts at North Austin and Dallas — is already closed by a new standard set this week.
  • We scaled safety with the business. +23.5% miles and a fleet full of new drivers, and the safety picture still improved across the board.

Events Retained for Coaching

Derived — replaces the Q1 coaching-volume pull (retired 2026-07-23)

Cameron's call, 7/23: coaching volume is retired as a standing packet metric. It existed in Q1 to prove a thesis — "we're dismissing less because we're coaching more" — and that thesis is now proven by two quarters of movement. From here the same story is carried by a number we already have, with no extra pull from Mike: events retained = total events minus dismissals. That is the volume of real, kept, coachable material the branches have to work with.

QuarterEventsDismissedRetainedRetention Rate
Q4 '251,056509 (48.2%)54751.8%

Read: we now keep and act on 79.3% of everything the cameras catch, up from barely half two quarters ago. Retained volume rose from 429 to 715 events even as the standard tightened — so there is materially more to coach against, not less.

⚠ Say "retained," not "coached." Events minus dismissals is the ceiling on coaching, not a count of coaching conversations. A retained event may have been coached, may still be sitting unreviewed, or may have been closed without a conversation — Samsara's coachingState is the only field that separates those, and we are deliberately no longer pulling it. Do not state or imply that 715 coaching conversations happened. Also note the Q4 and Q1 dismissal rates come from each quarter's own reported basis, so the trend is directionally sound but the denominators are not guaranteed identical across all three quarters.
